Playing Sports Lesson 1A

These are the key verbs we use when we talk about sports and games:

kick use your foot
hit with bat/racquet
throw with your hand (release)
catch receive with hands
pass give to teammate
shoot try to score
run move fast on feet
jump go up in the air

Match Results

win 🏆be the best / get 1st
draw (UK) / tie (USA) 🤝same score
lose 😞not win

Examples in context

In marathon running, it's important to have good shoes, otherwise you can hurt your knees when you run.
In tennis, don't look at your racquet when you hit the ball — look at where you want it to go.
In ice hockey, it's always better to pass the ball to your teammate if they are in a better position.
Remember: throw = you release the object (like a javelin); pass = you give it to a teammate; shoot = you aim at a goal to score.

Indefinite Pronouns Lesson 1B

We combine prefixes (every-, some-, no-) with suffixes (-body/-one, -where, -thing) to talk about people, places, and things in a general way.

PeoplePlacesThings
ALLeveryone / everybodyeverywhereeverything
ONEsomeone / somebodysomewheresomething
NONEno one / nobodynowherenothing

Examples

People everywhere like football — it's the most popular sport in the world. (= all places)
A racquet is something that you use to hit the ball in tennis. (= one thing)
In basketball, nobody is allowed to kick the ball. (= no person)
⚠️ Important Grammar Rule:
With everybody and nobody, always use a singular verb.

✅ Everybody is good at tennis.
❌ Everybody are good at tennis.

First Conditional Lesson 1C

Structure:
IF + present simple ,   will / might / can + infinitive

We use the first conditional to talk about a possible future situation and its consequence.

ModalMeaningExample
will / won't Prediction (certain) If I'm late, I'll miss the test.
might / might not Possibility (maybe) If I miss the test, my parents might take my PS away.
can / can't Opportunity / Ability If they take my PS, we can't play games this weekend!
⚠️ Common Mistake:
❌ If I will be late… → ✅ If I am late…
The IF clause uses present simple, NOT will.

More examples from the lesson

If you try to carry too much gold, you'll be too slow to escape the monsters.
If we find a horse, then we can follow it to the forest.
If we go into the abandoned house, we might be safe!

Describing Toys — Adjectives Lesson 1D

We use adjective pairs (opposites) to describe toys, games, and activities:

Positive ✅Negative ❌
fun — enjoyableboring — not interesting
safe — no dangerdangerous — can hurt you
simple — easy to understandcomplicated — hard to understand
relaxing — makes you calmstressful — makes you tense
fun vs funny:
fun = something you enjoy doing → "Playing football is really fun!"
funny = something that makes you laugh → "My sister told a really funny joke!"

Giving Opinions Lesson 1D

it looks + adjective → your opinion is based on what you SEE 👁️
it sounds + adjective → your opinion is based on what you HEAR or READ 👂

Examples

Wow, that looks fun. I want to play next! (= I can see it)
10,000 pieces?! This puzzle sounds complicated. (= someone told me)
Did you see the new roller coaster? It looks dangerous!
I don't want to go to the opera. It sounds boring to me!
If someone describes a game to you and you react → use sounds.
If you see a photo or video of a game and react → use looks.

Words to Connect Ideas Lesson 1E

Connecting SIMILAR ideas (+)

WordPositionExample
alsobefore the main verb / after beThis game is very popular and it is also great fun.
as wellend of the sentenceAdults can play it as well.
tooend of the sentenceThe game is easy, and it's fun too.
andbetween clausesIt can roll quickly, and it can jump.

Connecting DIFFERENT ideas (−)

WordPositionExample
howeverbeginning of a new sentence (+ comma)I love the game. However, some people say it has too many rules.
thoughend of the sentenceThey say it is boring. I think it's really fun, though.
butbetween clausesIt sounds simple, but there are lots of rules.
Quick Summary:
Similar ideas → also, as well, too, and
Different ideas → however, though, but
